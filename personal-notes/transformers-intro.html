Part 01
Why Did We Need Transformers?

Before transformers, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) — specifically LSTMs — were the go-to model for language tasks. But they had critical weaknesses that transformers were designed to solve.

🐢 RNN / LSTM (Old Way)

Processes One Word at a Time

RNNs iterate through words sequentially. Each word is processed, a "recurrent state" is updated, and that state is passed forward to the next word.

  • Sequential — cannot parallelise
  • Forgets early words in long sentences
  • Slow to train on large datasets
  • Struggles with long-term dependencies
⚡ Transformer (New Way)

Processes Entire Sequence at Once

Transformers calculate an attention score for every word against every other word — simultaneously — giving a full picture of the sentence in one pass.

  • Parallel processing — dramatically faster
  • Sees all words simultaneously — no forgetting
  • Handles long-term dependencies perfectly
  • BLEU score of 41 in just 3.5 days of training

Part 02
The Attention Mechanism

Attention is the secret ingredient. It lets the model ask: "When trying to understand this word, which other words matter most?" and assign weights accordingly.

Fig 1.3 — Attention scores when translating "speak" → "parle"

HIGH ATTENTION
speak → parle (direct translation)
MEDIUM ATTENTION
don't → parle (negation context)
LOW ATTENTION
I → parle (less relevant)
⚖️
Attention Scores
A number computed for every word-pair. Higher score = stronger relationship. These scores tell the model where to "focus" when encoding each word.
🎯
Context-Aware Encoding
Unlike an RNN's single context vector, each word in a transformer gets its own context-aware representation based on its attention scores with every other word.
🔀
Parallel Computation
All attention scores are calculated at once using matrix multiplication — no waiting for word 1 before computing word 2. This is why transformers are so fast.
Part 03
Multi-Head Attention

One attention head sees one type of relationship. Multi-head attention runs several attention heads in parallel — each learning a different aspect of the sentence — then combines their outputs.

Fig 1.4 — "The movie was not bad" — Multi-head detects the inversion

The movie was not 🔄 bad
→ Overall sentiment: POSITIVE ✓
An LSTM would likely see "bad" and classify as negative. Multi-head attention catches the "not" ↔ "bad" inversion relationship simultaneously, yielding the correct positive classification.
🔵
Head 1 — Syntax
Focuses on grammatical relationships: subject–verb–object structure, parts of speech.
🟣
Head 2 — Negation
Specialises in detecting negation words ("not", "never", "no") and their targets.
🟢
Head 3 — Sentiment
Attends to emotionally charged words and how they relate to the overall topic.
🟡
Head 4 — Co-reference
Tracks pronouns and references — "he", "it", "they" — back to the nouns they refer to.
Why Multi-Head Beats Single Attention
Multiple perspectives — each head learns different linguistic relationships independently.
Combined output — results from all heads are concatenated and projected, merging all insights.
BLEU score of 41 — the original transformer achieved this on English-to-French in only 3.5 days.

Part 05
When & Why To Use Transformers

Transformers aren't just for text — and advanced techniques like zero-shot and few-shot learning make them extremely versatile.

🎯
Zero-Shot Learning
The model handles tasks it was never explicitly trained for, using its broad pre-existing knowledge to infer and generalise. No task-specific training examples needed.
classify("This film was fantastic!", labels=["positive", "negative", "neutral"])
# → No labelled examples needed!
🔍
Few-Shot Learning
The model learns a new task from just a handful of examples, leveraging its pre-existing knowledge. 5–10 examples can outperform traditional models trained on thousands.
prompt = """Positive: "I loved it!"
Negative: "It was boring."
What is: "Not bad at all"? →"""
📝
NLP Tasks
Text classification, sentiment analysis, translation, summarisation, question answering, named entity recognition. The original stronghold of transformers.
🖼️
Vision (ViT)
Vision Transformers treat image patches like word tokens. Competing with and often surpassing CNNs on image classification tasks.
🎵
Audio & Multimodal
Transformers now underpin speech recognition (Whisper), music generation, and multimodal models that understand both text and images simultaneously.
⚠️ Limitations to Be Aware Of
Domain specificity — Billion-parameter models can underperform in specialised domains like healthcare or legal where domain-specific context is critical.
Computational cost — Large models (110M → 175B parameters) are expensive to deploy, especially in real-time systems requiring low latency.
Choose wisely — A smaller fine-tuned model often outperforms a giant general-purpose one on specific tasks, at a fraction of the cost.
Summary
Key Takeaways
  • ⚡ Transformers use attention + multi-head attention — spotlighting the most important words when encoding each part of a sentence, unlike RNNs that maintain a single recurrent state.
  • 🔀 Attention scores replace recurrent states — every word gets a rich context-aware representation rather than fighting for space in one fixed-size vector.
  • 👁️ Multi-head attention = multiple perspectives — several attention heads run in parallel, each learning different linguistic patterns (syntax, negation, coreference, etc.).
  • 🚀 Transformers handle long-term dependencies — equal access to all positions in the sequence means information from word 1 is never "forgotten" by word 100.
  • 📅 BLEU 41 in 3.5 days — equivalent state-of-the-art performance achieved in a tiny fraction of the training time previously required, revolutionising the field.
  • 🎯 Zero-shot & few-shot learning — advanced techniques that allow large pre-trained models to generalise to unseen tasks with zero or very few examples.
  • ⚠️ Limitations exist — billion-parameter models can struggle in specialised domains and have high compute/memory demands. Always choose the right-sized model for your task.
🎓

The Big Idea

The transformer's core innovation is elegantly simple: instead of processing sequences step-by-step and accumulating a single memory state, it asks "How does every word relate to every other word?" all at once. This attention-based view of language is so powerful it took the field from weeks of training to days — and gave rise to everything from ChatGPT to Gemini.
